    REFERRING PHYSICIAN
    Fawaz Alfarra, M.D.

    This is new patient visit to Neurologic Clinic at Boca Raton office. The patient came accompanied by relatives.

    ALLERGIES
    THE PATIENT DENIES ANY ALLERGIES.

    primary care physician
    Dr. Fawaz Alfarra.

    chief complaint
    Parkinsonism.

    history of present illness
    This is a 67-year-old right-handed male with past medical history remarkable for myasthenia gravis diagnosed in 2020. The patient was in usual state of health until 2021 when he started complaining of left eye ptosis. He was seen by a neurologist, who ordered an MRI of the brain which was unremarkable. Then, the patient started developing right hand stiffness, rigidity, clumsiness. The neurologist made diagnosis of myasthenia gravis. Of interest to mention, the acetylcholine receptor antibodies were negative. CT chest was normal. LP, EEG, EMG, and MRI of the brain were unremarkable. Mestinon provoked no improvement. During 2023, he started complaining of slowness of gait, more rigidity on the right side. No falls. In July 2023, he started having jealous delusions thinking that the wife has an affair with a nephew. The patient denied visual hallucinations. On October 2023, he was admitted to Memorial Hospital due to worsening of weakness, rigidity on the right side, and was diagnosed with dementia with Lewy bodies. On November 7, 2023, he was seen by Dr. Baumel at University of Miami. On March 20, 2023, he had a last visit with Dr. Baumel. The conclusion so far was that he may have corticobasal syndrome. Levodopa trial was given up to 2 tablets p.o. twice a day with no improvement. Donepezil was started 5 mg at bedtime.

    The patient comes today, referred for evaluation and treatment by Dr. Tarek Zakaria, chief of Neurology at Memorial Neuroscience Institute.

    review of systems
    Sense of smell is normal. No depression. No anxiety. In terms of cognition, there are mild word-finding difficulties, difficulty recalling names of objects at times. Comprehension is partially reduced. Visuospatial orientation is normal. There is mild forgetfulness. No psychosis. Positive hallucinations. Paranoid with jealous delusions with positive impulsivity and irritability. No orthostatic dizziness. No micrographia. Positive hypophonia. No constipation. Positive urinary urgency. No incontinence. No double vision. Positive dysphagia, which was evaluated with a modified barium swallowing test recently and was unremarkable.

Arising from chair is scored as 2, gait is scored as 2, freezing is scored as 1, posture instability scored as 1, posture scored as 2, global spontaneity movement scored as 2. No postural kinetic or resting tremor. Gait: The patient is able to arise from chair, pushing himself up from the chair. While walking, he has significant reduction on the arm swing bilaterally, reduction of stride length. Turning is very slow. Pull test, the patient takes 4 steps backwards, but recovers unaided. Posture is stooped. In addition, the patient has ideomotor apraxia on the right upper extremity. Frontal release signs: Positive palmomental, snouting, and glabellar signs.

    ancillary examination
    None.

    assessment and plan
    This is a 67-year-old right-handed male with:
    1. Parkinsonism, likely atypical stage 2.5 on the Hoehn and Yahr scale. The patient is having Parkinson symptomatology since 2021. The patient was given carbidopa/levodopa 200 mg p.o. twice day with no improvement in parkinsonism or walking. Of interest to mention that the patient has supranuclear ophthalmoplegia and also have ideomotor apraxia on the right upper extremity. All of the above are signs of tauopathies (a supranuclear ophthalmoplegia for PSP and an ideomotor apraxia for corticobasal syndrome. Of interest also to mention asymmetry on Parkinson symptomatology, which has been described on cases of corticobasal syndrome rather than progressive supranuclear palsy. In my opinion, the patient had a tauopathy with a combination of progressive supranuclear palsy and a corticobasal syndrome phenotype A. Regarding a cognitive dysfunction, personality changes, irritability, some word-finding difficulties, and jealous delusions and impulsivity, these could be features of frontotemporal dementia, also another tauopathy.
    2. Pseudobulbar affect: The patient has multiple episode of involuntary and pathological laughing during the office visit.
    3. Cognitive dysfunction, likely a frontotemporal dementia.
    4. Psychosis.
    5. Left ptosis of unclear etiology.
    6. Mild dysphagia.

    recommendations
    1. Neuropsychological testing to assess cognition.
    2. Physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy evaluation and treatment.
    3. Continue donepezil 10 mg at bedtime.
    4. Nuplazid 34 mg daily for psychosis.
    5. Patient to use a walker on 24/7 basis.
    6. Regarding diet, I advised soft mechanical diet for now.
    7. Swallowing precautions and fall precautions were given.
    8. The patient will return to clinic in 6 months.
