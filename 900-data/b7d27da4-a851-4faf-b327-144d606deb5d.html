    DIVISION OF PULMONARY CRITICAL CARE AND SLEEP MEDICINE

    CARDIO-PULMONARY STRESS TEST

    Patient Name: Johnny Henriquez
    Date of Birth: 9/8/1964
    BMI: Body mass index is 28.25 kg/m².
    Medical Record: 100746997
    Encounter Date: 9/5/2023

    Clinical History:
    59 y.o. male referred for stress testing for evaluation of Heart failure.

    Interpretation:
    Exercise:
    The patient exercised on the Cornell protocol for 4 minutes and 9 seconds.
    The patient achieved a max work level of 3.5 METS (1 MET = 3.5 ml O2/kg/min, light work 1.5-4 METS, moderate 3-6 METS, heavy work 5-15 METS).
    The test was stopped by the patient because of dyspnea 7/10 leg fatigue 4/10 dizziness 7/10.
    A total of 85 watts of work was conducted, which is reduced at 57% predicted.
    RER was 0.74 (normal RER VCO2/VO2 > 1.10), suggestive of sub-optimal effort.
    VO2 at AT was 27%. Anaerobic threshold was not reached.

    Cardiovascular response to exercise:
    Baseline heart rate (HR) was 80 beats per minute (bpm). The peak heart rate of 108 bpm, which is 67 % predicted (HR max predicted 220 - age) and reduced.
    Heart rate reserve was 54 (HRR = HR max predicted - peak HR = normal <15 beats/min).
    Heart rate decrease at 1 min recovery was 15 (peak HR - HR at 1 min recovery, normal >12 bpm, <12 bpm suggests heart failure).
    O2 pulse (VO2/HR) was normal at 11.5 mL/beat or 87 % predicted.
    Blood pressure was 122/86 at rest, which rose to 140/77 at peak exercise.
    Baseline EKG showed NSR with intraventricular conduction delay. Uninterpretable EKG tracing at peak exercise due to motion artifact.

    Ventilatory response to exercise:
    The maximal ventilation was 33 L/minute, which is 37 % predicted.
    Breathing reserve was 65%, which is high (Normal BR = MVV - peak VE >11 L or peak VE/MVV x100 <85%. Wide normal range: 72 +15%).
    The maximal respiratory rate was normal at 20 breaths per minute.
    Dead space decreased as expected.
    Oxygen saturation remained stable.
    Ventilatory equivalents for O2 and CO2 were elevated. The VE/CO2 anaerobic threshold was 37. Ventilatory Class: VE/CO2 I - < 29.9 excellent prognosis, II - 30-35.9 low risk of major cardiac event, III - 36-44.9 intermediate risk IV- >45 poor prognosis.
    Pre spirometry was normal.

    Gas exchange response to exercise:
    Relative peak VO2 was 15.7 mL/kg/min which is 58 % predicted and decreased. Weber Class in chronic heart failure: A >20 ml/kg/min (normal), B 16 -20 ml/kg/min (intermediate risk), C 10-16 ml/kg/min (intermediate risk), D <10 ml/kg/min (poor prognosis). Ventilatory class for unexplained exertional dyspnea: Class I - 100%, II - 77-99%, III - 50-75% and IV - <50%.
    Absolute peak VO2 was 1.2 L/min, which is 58 % predicted.
    End-tidal was monitored: PetO2 was normal and was PetCO2 normal.

    Impression:
    This was a submaximal study. Work achieved was suboptimal
    VO2 at AT was 27%, not reached (<40% predicted indicates circulatory impairment vs deconditioning) - interpret results with caution.
    The O2 pulse and BP response were normal. The HR response was blunted, suggesting chronotropic incompetence which may have been medication related. The VE/CO2 was elevated due to increased physiological dead space ventilation secondary to V/Q mismatch and diffusion impairment due to heart failure and/or pulmonary hypertension.
    The breathing reserve was high. The oxygen saturation and spirometry were within normal limits
    The peak VO2 was 15.7 mL/kg/min, 58% predicted. Weber Class in chronic heart failure: C 10-16 ml/kg/min (intermediate risk).
    Clinical correlation is advised.
