Description of Operative Procedure The patient was taken to the operating room and after positive identification IV sedation was administrated. The patient was placed in prone position in the operating table. Pillows were placed under the lower abdomen to flatten the sacrum. The C arm was draped and moved into AP position to provide fluoroscopic mapping of the sacral region which included marking out the midline of the sacrum, SI joints, sciatic notches, medial foraminal borders and sacral foramina. The C arm was moved to lateral position to image the area from sacral promontory to the coccyx. Local injection of 1% lidocaine was administered administer. 1st we proceeded to remove the old receiver. The old pocket was opened. Subcutaneous dissection was done down to the capsule. The capsule was calcified. We excised all the capsule. We removed the receiver and sent to pathology. We follow the track of the lead immuno counter incision on the close to the midline. We found the lead and removed the lead completely from the sacrum. This was done under direct vision. The lid was intact. Then we proceeded to place the new sacral nerve stimulator stage I and 2. A 20-gauge size foraminal needle was introduced approximately 2 cm above the sciatic notch and 2 cm lateral to sacrum midline. We were feeling for foraminal margins until the S3 foramen was identified and penetrated. The depth of the needle was confirmed and adjusted fluoroscopically. Proper needle position was confirmed by patient identification of location of sensation, direct observation of the lifting of the perineum or Bellows, and observation of plantar flexion of the great toe utilizing the external test stimulator. Then we used same technique to access the left lateral sacral midline and tested in same fashion through the right S3 foramina. We decided that the right side seemed to have a better response and we chose the right side for the lead placement. The foramen needle stylet was removed and directional guide was placed and confirmed fluoroscopically. The foramen needle was removed. An incision was made the referral to the directional guide through the fascial layer. The lead introducer sheath with dilator was placed over the directional guide and directed into the foramina ensuring the radiopaque marker of the lead introducer did not extend beyond the anterior edge of the sacrum. The dilator was unlocked and removed along with directional guide.The lead was then placed through the introducer sheath to the first white line. Position was checked fluoroscopically. The lead was then further introduced until 3 electrodes were visible below the sacrum. Each electrode was tested for location of patient sensation, visualization of Bellows, and plantarflexion of the great toe. After satisfactory position was confirmed the introducer thickness was retracted under continuous fluoroscopic control in the anterior leads lie into the presacral tissue. Another incision was made to create a subcutaneous pocket. I dissected into the subcutaneous tissue posterior to the iliac crest and lateral to the sacrum. Blunt dissection was done until the gluteal fascia was identified and hemostasis was achieved. A tunneling tool was placed from the lead exit site subcutaneously to the pocket site. The tunneling tool was removed and the lead was fed through the straw and pulled out of the pocket site. The leg was cleansed of bodily fluids and dried. The lead was completely dry. The normal receiver was connected to the lead and it was tightened with a hex wrench over the single screw. The receiver was placed on the pocket. The Medtronic rep tested the device and it was working fine. The wound was irrigated with antibiotic solution and water and closed with 3-0 Vicryl stitches for the subcutaneous tissue and 4-0 Monocryl in a subcuticular fashion for the skin. Dermabond was placed on top of the skin. Needle, instrument and sponges count was correct --2. The patient tolerated toward the procedure went out to the recovery area in a stable condition.
